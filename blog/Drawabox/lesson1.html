Lesson 1: Lines, Ellipses, and Boxes

This lesson is structured to teach students the mechanical and analytical skills necessary to be able to make consistent and appropriate marks on a page or canvas. It highlights the fact that trying to involve our brain too much during the act of making marks decreases the quality of the mark as our brain constantly tries to course-correct. Instead, it suggests that we let go of the desire for accuracy and prioritise flow, in the beginning stages, as accuracy is something that will come over time. Furthermore, this lesson stresses the fact that the ultimate goal of the exercises in this section is to offload the technical aspects of drawing to muscle memory and that students shouldn't get stuck trying to perfect each homework. The latter parts of the lesson focus on teaching the student some basic perspective concepts.

Exercises

Lines

This lesson teaches the fundamentals of markmaking. It starts by defining the three drawing pivots - wrist, elbow, and shoulder - and explains which pivot to use in different situations. The main takeaway is to make sure my marks are flowing smoothly and continuously while maintaining a consistent trajectory.

Super-Imposed lines

Purpose: This exercise is all about confidence. As you draw your superimposed strokes, you will notice that you're not going to be able to see where your pen is drawing, because your hand will be blocking it. This will make it particularly difficult to guide the stroke as you go.

Required repetitions: 2
Super-Imposed lines 2
Ghosted Lines

Purpose: This exercise is intended to introduce you to a technique that you will be using on every single mark you make through all of the Drawabox lessons (and hopefully beyond). The goal is to, over time, develop a habit of thinking before you draw. To consider what purpose the mark you're about to make serves and whether or not it is the best mark for that purpose, or if another existing one is already accomplishing the same task.

Required repetitions: 1
Ghosted Planes

Purpose: This exercise is about applying the ghosting method to something slightly more complex than a single line.

Required repetitions: 2
Ghosted Planes 2

Ellipses

Ellipses are extremely important and notoriously annoying to draw. They are found all over the place in mechanical drawings. Cars, space ships, tanks, machines - anything man-made will probably make extensive use of ellipses. This lesson builds on the principle of making smooth and continuous marks, and applies it to ellipses. It also describes the properties of ellipses as follows:

  • Scale - the overall size of the ellipse
  • Orientation - the angle at which it is positioned
  • Degree - effectively the width of the narrower dimension of the ellipse

Tables of Ellipses

Purpose: This exercise is meant to get you used to drawing ellipses, in a variety of sizes, orientations and degrees. It also sets out a clear space each ellipse is meant to occupy, giving us a means to assess whether or not an ellipse was successful, or if there were visible mistakes (where it went outside of its allotted space, or ended up falling short).

Required repetitions: 2
Table of Ellipses 2
Ellipses in Planes

Purpose: The purpose of this exercise is just about maintaining the smooth, even shape of the ellipses above all else. It's an exercise in calming down. The difficulties faced here are generally more of one's own making. Take it easy, and just try your best to drop a nice, even ellipse in the Planes.

Required repetitions: 2
Ellipses in Planes 2
Funnels

Purpose: This exercise is really about getting used to the use of the minor axis line, as well as continuing to work on getting those ellipses to fit snugly within a set space.

Required repetitions: 1

Boxes

A box consists of three sets of parallel lines. If you're familiar with three dimensional geometry, each set defines an axis - either x, y or z - and in doing so, it establishes the foundation of what we understand to be 3D space. Any object can be simplified into the box that encompasses it, any form can be represented and constructed within a box, and any box can be subdivided, carved, and built upon to create any complex object.

Plotted Perspective

Purpose: This exercise is really only geared at familiarizing those who are starting out with the concept of a vanishing point, and how we can plot lines that are parallel in 3D space back to their shared vanishing points.

Required repetitions: 1
Rough Perspective

Purpose: This exercise is all about separating you from the explicit reliance on plotting everything back to your vanishing point and getting used to the fact that your parallel lines converge. Due to the nature of perspective, with the innumerable vanishing points that may be necessary in a given scene, we can't necessarily rely on all of them being explicitly drawn out. Even though you've got mistakes in your estimations, the boxes still are likely to look pretty close to correct, and ultimately there are more important things than perfection when it comes to getting an idea across with visual means.

Required repetitions: 2
Rough Perspective 2
Rotated Boxes

Purpose: The rotated boxes exercise is also about learning to infer information about space and the arrangement of objects from neighbouring objects. Each box has others beside, above and below it, and by the time you're drawing one, some of these others have already been constructed. While their edges don't run perfectly parallel to one another, they do have a similar enough trajectory to some of the next box's edges to provide some important information.

Required repetitions: 1
Organic Perspective

Purpose: By diving into this exercise, you're being forced to contend with freely rotating boxes in 3D space without any real grounding of how to deal with them. There's no concrete vanishing points that you're marking out on the page, no neighbouring forms to base things off. It's more guesswork than you'll have dealt with till now. The point isn't to be able to nail this, but to get your gears turning as you start thinking about the fact that this is a kind of spatial problem you're facing.

Required repetitions: 2
Organic Perspective 2
